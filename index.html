About me

Welcome to my website! I am a third-year undergraduate student in physics at University of Malaya. I am interested in star formation at which I did my final year project on this topic. At University of Malaya, I did my final year project under the Radio Cosmology Research laboratory (RCRL) supervised by Prof. Dr. Zamri Bin Zainal Abidin, a prominent radio astronomer in Malaysia. I investigated the molecular outflows originated from the star forming regions with the radio data from ALMA in order to understand how the star formation can be triggered from the outflows. My other interests include other branches of optical astronomy and observational astrophysics.

I enjoy everything about astronomy. Whenever I have leisure time, I always use my telescope in my backyard to observe and capture images of fascinating objects in the night sky. I captured images of nebulae, galaxies, planets, and comets, and I share them on my Facebook page. I occasionally use my telescope to perform science studies; for instance, I look for moving asteroids by "blinking" through images of the sky. I also made plans for additional upcoming mini-projects, like measuring the light curve of binary stars and an exoplanet's transit right from my own backyard. I truly believe that everyone should have access to science, and that we can conduct similar research in our own backyard as is carried out by professional astronomers at observatories.

Along with my interests and hobbies, I gave my time as a volunteer for Apadilangit, a group that aims to promote knowledge of and participation in Malaysia's and the world's space programmes. Apadilangit is dedicated to fostering interest in science, technology, engineering, arts, and mathematics.

Project

As an undergraduate final year project, I devoted some time to investigate the 13C17O molecular radio emission data obtained by ALMA. I identify that the emission consists of two continuous flows of gas from the presence of two peaks of the shifted velocities in the molecular spectra that are opposing to each other suggesting that this outflow is a bipolar type, and the materials are being ejected at such a high velocity. It is usually associated with protostars (young, forming stars). After considering the properties of the outflows, this might suggest that the star formation may be induced and being regulated by the outflows.



Around July 2022, C/2017 K2 (PanSTARRS) comet made its closest approach to our planet. I took this opportunity to capture the comet images for around 1 hour. Using astrometry, I concluded that the comet velocity is around 37 km/sec relative to the observer on Earth.

Astrophotography

My Observatory

I have a Skywatcher 72ED Apochromatic refractor telescope with a 420 mm focal length. I had a variety of cameras, each with a specific use. The TEC cooled QHY 183C, which I use as my main imaging camera, is intended for deep-sky photography. I use Svbony SV305 Pro to image planets. A guide scope (Svbony SV106 Achromatic) and a guide camera (ZWO ASI 120mm mini) make up my guiding setup. All of these pieces of gear were mounted in equatorial mode on the AZ-GTi mount. ASCOM connection is used to establish a connection between the computer and the mount. Astrophotography Tool (APT), which I use to to control the imaging sessions semi-automatically, is my main imaging software. Other useful softwares that I use while imaging are Stellarium, Cartes Du Ciel, Sharpcap and PHD2 Guiding. For processing the data, I use Deep Sky Stacker, Siril and Photoshop.

According to data from lightpollutionmap.info, my observatory experiences light pollution that is in the Class 4 Bortle range, which is a transitional sky between rural and urban areas. Without utilising any light pollution filters, the sky is clear enough for me to image for a longer period of time.

In the long run, I am planning to construct a small observatory where I can conduct remote imaging sessions for each imaging session. I can operate my observatory remotely even though I am a long way away. Additionally, I intend to equip my observatory with an all-sky camera, which will be utilised to observe the sky from a wide angle and function as a meteor watch so that I might somehow promote space knowledge through public outreach. With that, I intend to open it up to the public so that anyone can use my observatory remotely and watch the night sky in real time.
